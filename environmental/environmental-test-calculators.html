Environmental Test Tools

Vibration and thermal calculators.

These are first-pass planning tools for environmental qualification work. They are intended to help size the problem, spot missing setup items and support engineering discussion before the formal procedure, facility data and customer requirements take over.

gRMS summary. The PSD plot shows where the vibration energy sits across frequency. The gRMS value is the square root of the area under that PSD curve, so it gives one overall severity number for the full random vibration profile.
  • Higher gRMS usually means a more severe overall random vibration test.
  • Two profiles can have the same gRMS but excite hardware differently if the energy is concentrated at different frequencies.
  • Use gRMS for quick comparison and shaker sizing, but always inspect the PSD shape and resonance behaviour.

What the different random vibration tests mean

The same PSD calculator can be used for several different verification jobs. The important difference is not the maths; it is the purpose of the test, the hardware being tested and the level/duration selected by the programme.

Flight limit level

This is the expected flight environment, often called the maximum expected level. It is usually predicted from launcher data, analysis, previous measurements or payload-level testing.

Use case: baseline environment for deriving qualification, acceptance or protoflight levels.

Qualification test

A higher-severity test used to show the design has margin over the expected flight environment. It is often run on a qualification model or representative hardware rather than every flight unit.

Use case: proving a new payload electronics box, bracket or subsystem design before it is accepted for flight build.

Acceptance test

A test applied to flight hardware to confirm that the built item is acceptable for use. It is normally less severe than qualification because the aim is to screen the item, not consume unnecessary life.

Use case: testing each flight up/down converter or avionics unit before delivery to the spacecraft integrator.

Protoflight test

A combined approach used when the same item is both the qualification article and the flight article. It usually applies qualification-style severity with carefully controlled duration and risk.

Use case: small satellite or one-off space hardware where building a separate qualification model is not practical.

Workmanship test

A screening test intended to expose build defects such as poor soldering, loose hardware, weak bonding, incorrect assembly or marginal internal connections.

Use case: applying a minimum random vibration screen to electronic, electromechanical or reworked hardware when detailed flight environment data is limited.

Retest or reflight test

A targeted repeat test considered after rework, disassembly, storage or previous flight exposure. The aim is usually to confirm workmanship and that the item is still suitable for use.

Use case: retesting a repaired unit, reassembled subsystem or hardware intended to fly again after modification.
