MIL-STD-461 FFT receiver

A pulsed signal only exists for microseconds at a time. Whether your receiver sees it depends on one thing: does the measurement dwell at each frequency last long enough to catch a pulse? This simulation recreates a CE102-style measurement and shows what a swept receiver and an FFT receiver each report against the limit line.

The rule this page exists to teach

A pulse train with repetition frequency PRF has one pulse every 1/PRF seconds. If a receiver dwells at a frequency for less time than that, there's a real chance no pulse arrives while it's listening — and it reports only noise. MIL-STD-461G's measurement-time requirements exist to prevent exactly this: the dwell at each frequency must be at least one pulse repetition period (and Table II sets minimum measurement times per band accordingly). An FFT receiver measures a whole block of frequencies simultaneously, so a single compliant dwell covers the block — which is how modern receivers meet the requirement dramatically faster than a stepped scan.

The dashed line is what the signal really contains. Tweak the four measurement times and watch each trace: where a dwell is shorter than the pulse period, that receiver misses pulses and its trace collapses into the noise floor — a failing signal can measure as a pass. Push any dwell past 1/PRF and its trace snaps up onto the true envelope. The classic demonstration: leave Swept A and FFT A short, make FFT B long, and compare.

The simulation is deliberately idealised: fixed 10 kHz–10 MHz span, representative resolution bandwidths, and a stylised CE102-shaped limit for context — the real limit depends on your application and voltage class. The lesson it demonstrates is exactly the one MIL-STD-461G's measurement-time rules encode; the numbers for a formal test come from the standard's Table II, not from here.
