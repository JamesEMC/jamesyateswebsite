Plane-wave equations

dBµV/m = 20 log10(E) + 120
E = 10(dBµV/m - 120) / 20
H = Eη0
dBµA/m = 20 log10(H) + 120
B = µ0H
pT = B × 1012
dBpT = 20 log10(pT)
S = E2η0 = E × H

Symbol key

E
Electric field strength, measured in volts per metre V/m.
H
Magnetic field strength, measured in amps per metre A/m.
B
Magnetic flux density, measured in tesla T.
η0
Impedance of free space, about 377 Ω. It links E and H for a plane wave.
µ0
Permeability of free space, used to link H to B.
pT
Picotesla. 1 pT = 10^-12 T.
S
Power density, measured in watts per square metre W/m².

In this equation set, S means power density. It is not siemens; siemens is the SI unit of conductance and is unrelated here.

Radiated power equations

E = √(30 × EIRP)d
EIRP = (E × d)230
EIRPdBm = 10 log10(EIRPW × 1000)
ERPdBm = EIRPdBm - 2.15
ERPW = EIRPW1.6406
dBµV/m at d = 20 log10(E) + 120

Symbol key

E
Electric field strength at the chosen measurement distance.
d
Distance from the radiator or antenna, measured in metres.
EIRP
Effective isotropic radiated power: power referenced to an ideal all-direction antenna.
ERP
Effective radiated power: power referenced to a half-wave dipole.
dBm
Power in decibels referenced to 1 mW.
30
Free-space far-field constant used in the ideal EIRP to field-strength relationship.
1.6406
Linear ratio equivalent to 2.15 dB, used to convert between EIRP and ERP.

What is deliberately not mixed in

Antenna-factor receiver readings, cable loss, preamp gain, detector type, bandwidth correction, scan dwell, and near-field probe factors belong in separate calculators. They are not pure field-unit conversions.

Plane-wave unit notes

V/m
Electric field strength. Think of it as the push on charge per metre. In EMC radiated testing, limits are often written as electric field strength.
dBµV/m
The same electric field on a decibel scale, referenced to 1 microvolt per metre. Useful because EMC levels can cover a very large range.
A/m
Magnetic field strength. In a plane wave it is tied to the electric field by the impedance of free space: E = η0H.
dBµA/m
Magnetic field strength on a decibel scale, referenced to 1 microamp per metre.
T
Tesla, the SI unit for magnetic flux density B. It describes how strong the magnetic field is in terms of magnetic flux per area.
G
Gauss, an older magnetic flux density unit. It is still seen in some fields. 1 T = 10,000 G.
pT
Picotesla. This is a very small magnetic flux density unit, useful when magnetic fields are tiny.
dBpT
Picotesla on a decibel scale, referenced to 1 pT.
W/m²
Power density. It says how much electromagnetic power passes through one square metre.
mW/cm²
The same idea as W/m², but in milliwatts per square centimetre. It is often used in RF exposure discussions.

Radiated-power unit notes

EIRP
Effective isotropic radiated power. It is the power an ideal all-direction antenna would need to produce the same field in the strongest direction.
ERP
Effective radiated power. Similar to EIRP, but referenced to a half-wave dipole rather than an ideal isotropic antenna. EIRP ≈ ERP + 2.15 dB.
dBm
A decibel power unit referenced to 1 milliwatt. 30 dBm = 1 W.
Distance
Field strength falls as distance increases because the same power spreads over a larger area. This page uses the ideal free-space far-field model.
Power density
In the far field, power density relates to electric field by S = E² / η0. This links the field value to energy flow through an area.
Far field
A region far enough from the antenna that the wave behaves more like a travelling plane wave. Close to the antenna, E and H may not follow the simple plane-wave relationship.
