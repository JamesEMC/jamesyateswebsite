Space Programme Test Experience

Environmental Qualification Testing

Space hardware has to survive environments that cannot be fully represented by normal bench testing. I have witnessed and supported environmental qualification testing across vibration, thermal and thermal-vacuum campaigns, with a strong focus on space and aerospace programmes.

My experience has included work alongside environmental test teams at major aerospace primes, commercial test houses and defence/aerospace engineering departments. The common thread has been the practical reality of test: the procedure, the chamber or shaker, the instrumentation, the fixture, the harnessing, the customer requirements and the evidence all have to come together before the result means anything.

Vibration Testing

I have witnessed sine, random and mechanical shock testing on space payload electronics, including up/down converter equipment tested at qualification and acceptance levels.

  • Fixture mounting and axis changes
  • Control and response accelerometers
  • Low-level sine sweeps and resonance searches
  • Random vibration, notching and dwell
  • Pre-test and post-test comparison

Thermal Testing

I have supported and witnessed thermal cycling on space and aeronautical products, including chamber checks, hot and cold exposure, dwell periods and functional testing at temperature.

  • Hot and cold plateaus
  • Dwell and soak timing
  • Thermocouple monitoring
  • Current draw and self-test checks
  • Functional performance at extremes

TVAC Testing

My TVAC experience includes chamber checks and instrumentation for space hardware, including a solar array segment for a geostationary satellite programme and a processor installed in a small thermal-vacuum chamber.

  • Pump-down and leak checks
  • Vacuum level and pressure monitoring
  • Heater zones and thermal shrouds
  • Feedthroughs and harness support
  • Oxygen depletion safety awareness

Practical Lesson

One of the most useful lessons I have seen came from a vibration test where the test fixture failed rather than the unit under test. A crack appeared in the fixture, a replacement was sourced, and the spare failed in the same location. The issue was eventually traced back to a weld that had not been completed in accordance with the correct standard.

The result was lost time, added pressure and a reminder that support equipment must be treated with the same care as the equipment under test. The fixture, harnessing, instrumentation and chamber or shaker setup all form part of the evidence chain.

Standards Context

My experience sits alongside the environmental verification approach described in standards such as NASA GEVS / GSFC-STD-7000, ECSS-E-ST-10-03, MIL-STD-810 and RTCA DO-160.

Environmental qualification testing has taught me that successful test campaigns depend on much more than the headline test level. Analysis is essential, but well-planned physical testing remains one of the most effective ways to reduce programme risk before flight.
